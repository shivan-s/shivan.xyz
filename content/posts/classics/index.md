I would say I loved reading. Even as a kid, I would consider reading an academic exercise. It's like eating your vegetables. You know it's good for your but it's not something you enjoy in the moment.

School probably didn't help. It made reading something you had to do.

As I write this now, I envy the bookworms who seem to comprehend and devour books, even the most verbose and hard-to-read texts out there. Why can't that be me?

I didn't read Harry Potter until my mid-twenties (I got stuck on Order of the Pheonix, by the way). Years into my work life, I rarely picked up any books. One that came to mind was [Yeonmi Park's _In Order To Live_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yeonmi_Park). This came to an end when I discovered self-help.

That lead me down a rabbit hole:
- _Atomic Habits_ by James Clear
- _Seven Habits of Highly Effective People_  by Stephen R. Covey

...just to name a few. I also read books on historical events like _Into Thin Air_ by  Jon Krakauer. But you can see my diet is non-fiction and self-help.

Before I continue, I want to say that reading self-help is fine. But the major realisation for me was that I had no real thoughts of my own. Though I was learning, I wasn't exercising my mind to think on it's own. That's the danger of non-fiction. Not in the sense, you question the facts for sure; but fiction is where you need to interpret - you need to think on your own.

I think it's a good idea to broaden the horizons. I want to read **and enjoy** classic literature.

And where am I going to start?

With _The Odyssey_ by "Homer".

This is a tough one, since _The Odyssey_ was created during the time when there was no novel or written text, and it certainly wasn't written in plain modern, modern English.
